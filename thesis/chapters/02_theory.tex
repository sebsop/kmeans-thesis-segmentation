\section{Classical K-Means Clustering and Spatial Weighting}
K-Means is a standard clustering algorithm that groups $N$ data points into $K$ uniform clusters \cite{lloyd1982least}. The algorithm works by iteratively minimizing WCSS \cite{krzanowski1988criterion}:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:wcss}
J = \sum_{i=1}^{K} \sum_{\mathbf{x} \in S_i} \|\mathbf{x} - \boldsymbol{\mu}_i\|^2
\end{equation}
where $S_i$ is the set of points in the $i$-th cluster, and $\boldsymbol{\mu}_i$ is the centroid of $S_i$. Each iteration has two main steps:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Assignment Step:} Every point $\mathbf{x}$ is assigned to its nearest centroid based on Euclidean distance: $S_i^{(t)} = \{ \mathbf{x} : \|\mathbf{x} - \boldsymbol{\mu}_i^{(t)}\|^2 \le \|\mathbf{x} - \boldsymbol{\mu}_j^{(t)}\|^2 \ \forall j \}$.
    \item \textbf{Update Step:} New centroids are calculated by averaging all points in each cluster: $\boldsymbol{\mu}_i^{(t+1)} = \frac{1}{|S_i^{(t)}|} \sum_{\mathbf{x} \in S_i^{(t)}} \mathbf{x}$.
\end{enumerate}
These steps repeat until the centroids stop changing or the algorithm reaches a maximum iteration limit ($I_{\text{max}}$) \cite{lloyd1982least}.

\subsection{The 5D Color-Spatial Manifold}\label{subsect:manifold}
Traditional clustering operates solely in 3D color spaces (such as RGB, HSV, or CIELAB) \cite{gonzalez2009digital,szeliski2022computer}. However, grouping pixels only by color causes spatial fragmentation, where pixels of similar color are grouped together regardless of their physical location in the image. To ensure spatial consistency, the feature space is extended to a 5D manifold \cite{comaniciu2002mean,achanta2012slic}:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{x} = [B, G, R, x, y]^T
\end{equation}
where $B, G, R$ represent color channels, and $x, y$ are the pixel coordinates.

Since color values and spatial coordinates have completely different scales, the pixel coordinates would dominate the distance formula, ignoring color differences \cite{bishop2006pattern}. To balance this, a spatial weight parameter ($\lambda_{\text{spatial}}$) is added \cite{shi2000normalized}: $\|\mathbf{x}_n - \boldsymbol{\mu}_i\|^2 = (B_n - \boldsymbol{\mu}_{i,B})^2 + (G_n - \boldsymbol{\mu}_{i,G})^2 + (R_n - \boldsymbol{\mu}_{i,R})^2 + \lambda_{\text{spatial}} \left[ (x_n - \boldsymbol{\mu}_{i,x})^2 + (y_n - \boldsymbol{\mu}_{i,y})^2 \right]$. A high $\lambda_{\text{spatial}}$ creates very compact, regular shapes, while a low value lets the engine prioritize color boundaries \cite{achanta2012slic}.

\subsection{Real-World Vision Applications and Edge Computing Constraints}
Real-time 5D clustering applies to computer vision tasks such as lane tracking in autonomous vehicles \cite{janai2020computer}, obstacle detection \cite{redmon2016you}, and surgical assistance overlays \cite{pham2000current}. In automated vision loops, standard display interfaces refresh at $60\text{ FPS}$ ($16.67\text{ ms}$ processing budget), while high-speed driving simulators require at least $120\text{ FPS}$ ($8.33\text{ ms}$ processing budget) to maintain immersion and low latency \cite{cruden2019simulator}. Virtual reality headsets similarly require sub-10 ms latency to prevent tracking oscillation, which corresponds to frame rates of at least 100 FPS \cite{jerald2009scene}.

Running large deep learning models on embedded devices consumes significant power and introduces excessive latency \cite{paszke2016enet}. Lightweight, GPU-accelerated 5D clustering serves as an efficient preprocessing step \cite{farivar2008parallel}, grouping pixels quickly before sending compressed data to downstream algorithms.

\subsection{Temporal Redundancy and Warm-Starting in Video Clustering}
When K-Means is applied to a continuous video feed, executing the clustering algorithm from scratch on every frame is highly inefficient. In standard video streams, the physical objects and lighting conditions change gradually between consecutive frames, a property known as temporal redundancy \cite{szeliski2022computer}. If the clustering engine initializes its centroids randomly (or via the expensive K-Means++ protocol \cite{arthur2007k}) on every frame, the computational complexity scales linearly with the number of Lloyd's iterations required to reach convergence.

To optimize this process, a warm-start strategy is implemented. Instead of selecting fresh starting seeds, the converged centroids from frame $t-1$ are used as the starting seeds for frame $t$. Because the spatial and color features of the pixels shift only marginally between frames, the cached centroids are already positioned close to their optimal locations. This temporal warm-start significantly reduces the iteration count needed for convergence, lowering the computational complexity per frame and enabling high-speed processing on live video feeds.

\subsection{Mathematical Clustering Quality Metrics}\label{subsect:metrics_def}
Three standard metrics are used to evaluate the clustering results: WCSS \cite{krzanowski1988criterion}, the Davies-Bouldin Index \cite{davies1979cluster, xiao2017davies}, and the Silhouette Coefficient \cite{rousseeuw1987silhouettes, shutaywi2021silhouette}.

\subsubsection{Within-Cluster Sum of Squares (WCSS)}
As defined in Equation~\ref{eq:wcss}, WCSS is the active objective function minimized by the classical K-Means algorithm during execution \cite{lloyd1982least}. While WCSS measures how compact a cluster is, it naturally decreases as more clusters $K$ are added \cite{krzanowski1988criterion}. In this framework, WCSS is used as a core metric to evaluate the vector quantization efficiency of both engines, quantifying how well the centroids represent and compress the original high-dimensional feature space with minimal distortion \cite{lloyd1982least}. Because WCSS does not measure how well-separated different clusters are from each other, the Davies-Bouldin Index \cite{xiao2017davies,davies1979cluster} and the Silhouette Coefficient \cite{shutaywi2021silhouette,rousseeuw1987silhouettes} are also implemented as additional telemetry diagnostics to compare the overall segmentation quality between the classical and quantum-inspired engines.

\subsubsection{The Davies-Bouldin Index}
The Davies-Bouldin Index ($\mathcal{D}\mathcal{B}$) evaluates clustering by checking the ratio of cluster size to the distance between cluster centers \cite{xiao2017davies,davies1979cluster}. We define cluster dispersion as $s_i = \left( \frac{1}{|S_i|} \sum_{\mathbf{x} \in S_i} \|\mathbf{x} - \boldsymbol{\mu}_i\|^2 \right)^{1/2}$ and centroid distance as $d(\boldsymbol{\mu}_i, \boldsymbol{\mu}_j) = \|\boldsymbol{\mu}_i - \boldsymbol{\mu}_j\|$. The DB index is the average similarity ratio across clusters: $\mathcal{D}\mathcal{B} = \frac{1}{K} \sum_{i=1}^{K} \max_{j \neq i} \left( \frac{s_i + s_j}{\|\boldsymbol{\mu}_i - \boldsymbol{\mu}_j\|} \right)$. A lower score means better, more separated clusters.

\subsubsection{The Silhouette Coefficient and Monte Carlo Approximation}
The Silhouette Coefficient $s(\mathbf{x}) \in [-1, 1]$ evaluates how well each point fits its cluster \cite{rousseeuw1987silhouettes,shutaywi2021silhouette}. For a point $\mathbf{x}$, its average distance to points in the same cluster ($a(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{|S_A| - 1} \sum_{\mathbf{y} \in S_A, \mathbf{y} \neq \mathbf{x}} d(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y})$) and its distance to the nearest neighboring cluster ($b(\mathbf{x}) = \min_{C \neq A} \left( \frac{1}{|S_C|} \sum_{\mathbf{z} \in S_C} d(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{z}) \right)$) are calculated, yielding the score: $s(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{b(\mathbf{x}) - a(\mathbf{x})}{\max(a(\mathbf{x}), b(\mathbf{x}))}$, where $d(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y})$ is the Euclidean distance between points $\mathbf{x}$ and $\mathbf{y}$. The global score is the average across all points.

\label{subsect:silhouette_complexity}
Calculating this metric exactly for a full VGA frame ($N = 307,200$ pixels) is computationally impractical for an interactive application context. Because the Silhouette score requires finding the pairwise distance between every single pixel, the total number of operations scales quadratically as $\frac{N(N-1)}{2}$, yielding $\frac{307,200 \times 307,200}{2} \approx 47.19$ billion distance calculations per frame. Executing roughly 47 billion calculations in a real-time $120\text{ FPS}$ or more pipeline (with a strict budget of $8.33\text{ ms}$ or less) is impossible. To bypass this bottleneck, a Monte Carlo approximation is used, sampling a random, uniform subset of $M=2,000$ pixels \cite{shutaywi2021silhouette}. This reduces the complexity to $\mathcal{O}(M^2)$ pairwise checks within the subset, which runs in less than a millisecond on a background thread \cite{williams2019c++}, while remaining highly accurate.

\section{Parallel Computing and GPU Architectures via CUDA}

\subsection{Computational Complexity Constraints of Sequential Architectures}
Running K-Means sequentially on a CPU is slower than on a GPU for real-time video \cite{farivar2008parallel}. For the same VGA frame size ($N = 307,200$), executing $K=8$ clusters over $I=15$ iterations requires the CPU to perform over $36.8$ million calculations per frame ($N \times K \times I = 36,864,000$). To achieve the target frame rate of $120\text{ FPS}$, the CPU must complete this in under $8.33\text{ ms}$, which is very difficult due to memory latency and single-core bottlenecks \cite{cook2012cuda}.

\subsection{The CUDA Execution Paradigm and Thread Hierarchy}
To solve this, calculations are offloaded to the GPU using CUDA \cite{CudaGuide}. CUDA uses the SIMT model, allowing thousands of threads to process different pixels at the same time \cite{cook2012cuda}. The GPU organizes these threads into a hierarchy \cite{CudaGuide}:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Grids and Blocks:} Launching a kernel initializes a \textit{Grid}, divided into \textit{Thread Blocks}. Threads in the same block run on the same Streaming Multiprocessor (SM), allowing them to share low-latency local cache and synchronize using hardware barriers \cite{CudaGuide,cook2012cuda}.
    \item \textbf{Warps:} At the hardware level, threads execute in groups of $32$ called \textit{Warps} \cite{cook2012cuda}. If threads in a warp take different execution paths (like an \texttt{if-else} branch), they serialize \cite{cook2012cuda}. This warp divergence reduces GPU utilization, so keeping thread execution paths uniform is a key goal \cite{cook2012cuda}.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{GPU Memory Hierarchy and Optimization Strategies}
Efficiently utilizing the GPU requires managing its multi-tiered memory to avoid execution stalls \cite{cook2012cuda}:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Global Memory:} Main VRAM serves as the primary storage. Coalesced memory transactions allow threads in a warp to access contiguous memory blocks in a single transaction, minimizing latency \cite{CudaGuide, cook2012cuda}.
    \item \textbf{Shared Memory (\texttt{\_\_shared\_\_}):} This on-chip, low-latency cache layer resides on the SM. Caching 5D cluster centroids here avoids the high latency of repeated global memory access \cite{CudaGuide, farivar2008parallel}.
    \item \textbf{Registers:} These private, zero-latency storage spaces serve each thread. High register usage per thread limits block concurrency on the SM, which reduces overall GPU occupancy \cite{CudaGuide, cook2012cuda}.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Parallel Strided Downsampling Theory}\label{subsect:downsampling_theory}
To eliminate CPU-bound preprocessing bottlenecks and achieve deterministic execution latency, a strided downsampling preprocessor is implemented directly in the CUDA kernel. Instead of using CPU resizing, the GPU downsamples the image as it is loaded.

For a stride $S \in [1, 16] \subset \mathbb{Z}^+$, a naive implementation would launch a thread for every pixel and skip inactive ones using a modulo check: $\text{Active}(idx) = (idx \pmod{S} == 0)$. This causes severe warp divergence \cite{cook2012cuda} and slow, uncoalesced memory reads \cite{CudaGuide}.

For this downsampling execution, the GPU grid is scaled to the exact downsampled size $W_{\text{out}} = \lceil W/S \rceil$ and $H_{\text{out}} = \lceil H/S \rceil$. Each thread $(x_{\text{out}}, y_{\text{out}})$ maps directly to the input array at $x_{\text{in}} = x_{\text{out}} \cdot S$ and $y_{\text{in}} = y_{\text{out}} \cdot S$. This ensures all threads are active and eliminates divergence \cite{CudaGuide, cook2012cuda}.

\section{Quantum Machine Learning and NISQ Limitations}

\subsection{The NISQ Era and Physical Hardware Constraints}
Quantum computing offers theoretical speedups, but current hardware is in the NISQ era \cite{preskill2018quantum}. These processors are limited by few qubits, short coherence times ($T_1, T_2$), and high gate error rates \cite{bharti2022noisy,koch2020demonstrating}. Deep quantum circuits accumulate too much noise to be useful, limiting the depth of circuits that can be executed reliably.

\subsection{Hybrid Classical-Quantum Algorithms}
To work around these limits, hybrid classical-quantum machine learning algorithms (such as the Variational Quantum Eigensolver (VQE) or the Quantum Approximate Optimization Algorithm (QAOA)) offload part of the work to a classical CPU or GPU \cite{mccaskey2018hybrid}. For clustering, the QPU calculates distances, while the classical processor updates the centroids, as demonstrated in algorithms like $q$-means \cite{kerenidis2019q, lloyd2013quantum}. This hybrid loop allows using quantum representations without exceeding the physical hardware limits. \cite{preskill2018quantum}

\subsection{The Real-Time API and Simulation Latency Bottleneck}
However, using a real QPU or a python simulator in a live video pipeline is impossible due to massive bottlenecks:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{State Preparation Overhead:} Encoding pixel features into qubits requires complex state preparation circuits (e.g., amplitude encoding), which scales poorly and exceeds the real-time frame window \cite{bharti2022noisy, kerenidis2019q}.
    \item \textbf{API and Network Latency:} Sending circuits to a remote QPU introduces network overhead, job queue delays, and transpilation latency, taking hundreds of milliseconds or seconds \cite{fraunhofer2025quantum}. This violates real-time frame pacing, which is quantified by metrics like Circuit Layer Operations Per Second (CLOPS) \cite{wack2021qualityspeedscalekey}.
    \item \textbf{Quantum Measurement Sampling Delay:} Quantum measurements must be repeated thousands of times (shots) to reconstruct stable probabilities, introducing execution latency that scales with the number of samples required to reduce shot noise \cite{bharti2022noisy}.
\end{itemize}
Since real QPUs and cloud-based simulators are too slow, quantum behaviors are simulated deterministically using parallelized GPU kernels.

\section{Quantum-Inspired Computing and the SWAP Test}

\subsection{Quantum Overlap and the SWAP Test Circuit}
To get the mathematical benefits of quantum computing without physical hardware latency, the quantum SWAP test is simulated directly on the GPU. In quantum information theory, the SWAP test measures the overlap (similarity) between two states $\lvert\psi\rangle$ and $\lvert\phi\rangle$ using three registers: an ancilla qubit initialized to $\lvert 0 \rangle_{\text{anc}}$, and two target registers holding the inputs \cite{buhrman2001quantum}. A Hadamard gate is applied to the ancilla, a Controlled-SWAP (CSWAP) gate swaps the targets if the ancilla is in state $\lvert1\rangle$, and a second Hadamard gate induces interference \cite{buhrman2001quantum}.

\subsection{Step-by-Step Mathematical Derivation}
The registers evolve as follows: the initial state is $\lvert\Psi_0\rangle = \lvert 0 \rangle_{\text{anc}}\lvert\psi\rangle\lvert\phi\rangle$. The first Hadamard gate yields the superposition $\lvert\Psi_1\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\lvert0\rangle_{\text{anc}}\lvert\psi\rangle\lvert\phi\rangle + \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\lvert1\rangle_{\text{anc}}\lvert\psi\rangle\lvert\phi\rangle$. The CSWAP gate swaps the target registers if the ancilla is $\lvert1\rangle$, giving $\lvert\Psi_2\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\lvert0\rangle_{\text{anc}}\lvert\psi\rangle\lvert\phi\rangle + \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\lvert1\rangle_{\text{anc}}\lvert\phi\rangle\lvert\psi\rangle$. A second Hadamard gate results in \cite{buhrman2001quantum}:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:psi_3}
\lvert\Psi_3\rangle = \frac{1}{2}\lvert0\rangle_{\text{anc}}(\lvert\psi\rangle\lvert\phi\rangle + \lvert\phi\rangle\lvert\psi\rangle) + \frac{1}{2}\lvert1\rangle_{\text{anc}}(\lvert\psi\rangle\lvert\phi\rangle - \lvert\phi\rangle\lvert\psi\rangle)
\end{equation}

According to Born's Rule, the probability $P(\lvert 0 \rangle)$ of measuring the ancilla in the ground state is:
\begin{align}
P(\lvert0\rangle) &= \left\| \langle 0 \rvert \Psi_3 \rangle \right\|^2 = \left\| \frac{1}{2}(\lvert\psi\rangle\lvert\phi\rangle + \lvert\phi\rangle\lvert\psi\rangle) \right\|^2 \\
&= \frac{1}{4} \left[ \langle\psi\vert\psi\rangle\langle\phi\vert\phi\rangle + 2\lvert\langle\psi\vert\phi\rangle\rvert^2 + \langle\phi\vert\phi\rangle\langle\psi\vert\psi\rangle \right] = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{2}\lvert\langle\psi\vert\phi\rangle\rvert^2
\end{align}
This proves that the ground state probability is directly related to the state overlap (or fidelity) $\lvert\langle\psi\vert\phi\rangle\rvert^2$ \cite{buhrman2001quantum}.

\subsection{Hilbert Phase Space Mapping and Implemented Metrics}\label{subsect:phasespace}
This state overlap is emulated directly in CUDA C++ without executing virtual quantum gates. The normalized 5D pixel features are mapped to phase states: $\theta_d = (p_d - c_d) \cdot \lambda_{\text{scale}}$, where $p_d$ is the pixel coordinate or color component, $c_d$ is the centroid coordinate, and $\lambda_{\text{scale}}$ is a scaling factor. This is a form of angle/phase encoding commonly used in quantum image representation \cite{yang2013novel}.

The 1D overlap is $\text{Overlap}_d = \cos^2(\theta_d)$. The global similarity is the product of these overlaps, representing the state overlap of the multi-qubit product state \cite{buhrman2001quantum}: $\text{Fidelity}(\mathbf{p}, \mathbf{c}) = \prod_{d=1}^{D} \cos^2\left( (p_d - c_d) \cdot \lambda_{\text{scale}} \right)$. Since K-Means requires a minimizing distance metric, this similarity is subtracted from unity:
\begin{equation}
D_{\text{Quantum}}(\mathbf{p},\mathbf{c}) = 1.0 - \prod_{d=1}^{D} \cos^2\left( (p_d - c_d) \cdot \lambda_{\text{scale}} \right)
\end{equation}
The scaling coefficient is locked to $\lambda_{\text{scale}} = \texttt{SCALE\_FACTOR} \cdot \texttt{PHASE\_OFFSET} = \frac{\pi}{2} \cdot 0.5 = \frac{\pi}{4}$. This bounds the angle within $[0, \frac{\pi}{4}]$, allowing the GPU to calculate the cosine quickly using hardware-accelerated instructions (\texttt{\_\_cosf}) \cite{CudaGuide}.

\subsection{Scientific Justification of Phase Boundaries}\label{subsect:boundary_justification}
Using this non-linear trigonometric distance instead of the standard Euclidean norm alters the optimization gradient structure for video segmentation. Because classical Euclidean distance scales linearly with feature displacement, gradual shadows or noise can cause pixels to bleed across cluster boundaries, failing to segment contiguous regions cleanly \cite{gonzalez2009digital, szeliski2022computer}.

By comparison, the quantum-inspired metric changes along a squared cosine curve, resembling the mathematical formulation of kernel-based projection methods that transform data into high-dimensional Hilbert spaces to resolve complex boundaries \cite{scholkopf1998nonlinear}. As the distance between a pixel and a centroid increases, the slope of $\cos^2(\theta)$ becomes steeper, yielding a sharp transition profile near the cluster borders. This steep gradient is formulated to suppress color-space bleeding, helping to stabilize cluster centers and preserve edge transitions in noisy regions \cite{shi2000normalized, achanta2012slic}. While empirical results (detailed in Chapter~\ref{chap:results}) show that both backends converge to functionally equivalent visual boundaries under static conditions due to the monotonic nature of the phase bounds, this non-linear mapping provides a mathematically robust framework for projecting spatial-color features directly onto quantum representations. This mathematical foundation directly informs the structural design and class interactions of the software architecture detailed in the next chapter.
